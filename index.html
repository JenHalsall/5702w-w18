About the Greenbelt

A sprawling crescent of fields and forests, the Greenbelt is composed of approximately 200 square kilometers of federally-owned land in the city of Ottawa, Ontario. Created in 1958 as a result of Jacques Greber's Plan for Canada's Capital, it was intended to limit urban sprawl, allocate a supply of land for future institutional use, and preserve a selection of greenspace within easy reach of the downtown core.

The lands now comprising the majority of the Greenbelt originally belonged to the townships of Nepean and Gloucester, which have since been amalgamated into Ottawa. In Gloucester, the affected area was overwhelmingly rural, occupied by family farms and small businesses. Landowners were given a choice: sell willingly to the National Capital Commission, the agency responsible for implementing Greber's vision in the National Capital Region; or face expropriation. As land was relinquished, the Commission proposed a new arrangement for affected landowners whose properties were not slated to be immediately re-purposed. Former owners were offered short-term leases and the opportunity to become tenants on their own land.

Although early plans for the Greenbelt proposed a largely agricultural focus, a practice of reforestation and naturalization soon developed. Large tracts of agricultural land were re-classified as conservation areas. While intensive ecologically-focused activities did occur at several sites, many fields were simply left to gradually grow over. Homes, businesses, and outbuildings were also removed from the landscape.

Today's Greenbelt appears radically changed when compared to its likeness 50 years ago. A wealth of public conservation and recreation areas are maintained, while agriculture and habitation have become secondary distinctions. The Commission now maintains approximately 85 farmsteads for lease, and an estimated 1,000 individuals live within the bounds of this green expanse.

This is a studio course. We will spend very little time in class on readings, as such; when we do discuss such things, it will be to provide context for what we are doing. I’m inspired very much by the work of historian Bill Turkle, who says to his own students in a similar kind of studio-led course,

Most of your in-class time will be devoted to experimenting with various kinds of technologies and learning how to apply them. It is playful and open-ended, and most of the drive has to come from you. I provide tools and equipment, I can show you how it works and suggest ways you might use it, and if you get stuck I can help you figure out a way forward.

That goes for us too.

This can be disappointing if you are expecting a more traditional arrangement. If you want to learn how to do computational analysis of historical texts, I’d suggest the self-directed version of Crafting Digital History would be more appropriate for you. But in this class, we’re doing something very different.

If you have questions or concerns, get in touch right away.

Do I need to be ‘techy’?

What does that mean, ‘techy’? It’s worth pondering. Short answer: no. If you can write an email, there’s nothing we’ll do in this course that is beyond you.

But what happens if something doesn’t work?

That is, in many ways, part of the point. Things will break. It’s when things break that we discover how best they can serve the broader goal. You will keep track of what you do, what you’re thinking, as you experiment and push yourself.

Glorious Failure is as valuable as Glorious Success

An ambitious project that succeeds is just as important to me as an ambitious project that fails spectacularly: both earn high grades! Document everything, and swing for the bleachers. That is the recipe for success. You’re welcome to play it safe, but know that playing it safe leads to lower grades.
